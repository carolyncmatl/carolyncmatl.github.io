STReSSD: Sim-To-Real from Sound for Stochastic Dynamics

Sound is an information-rich medium that captures dynamic physical events. In this work, we present the use of sound to bridge the gap between simulation and reality for dynamic objects, demonstrated for the canonical case of bouncing balls. A physically-motivated noise model is presented to capture stochastic behavior of the balls upon collision with the environment. A likelihood free Bayesian inference framework is used to infer the parameters of the noise model, as well as a material property called the coefficient of restitution, from real-world audio observations. A calibrated stochastic simulator is then used to learn a probabilistic model of ball dynamics. The predictive capabilities of the dynamics model are tested in two robotic experiments. In the first, open-loop predictions anticipate probabilistic success of bouncing a ball into a cup. The second experiment integrates audio perception with a robotic arm to track and deflect a bouncing ball in real-time. We envision that this work can serve as a critical step towards integrating audio-based inference into dynamic robotic environments..
